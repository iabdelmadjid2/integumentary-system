Anatomy of the Integumentary System

Understanding the Complex Structure of Skin, Hair, Nails, and Glands

Skin Layers

The skin has three main layers: epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis. The epidermis is the outermost layer, mostly made of dead, keratin-rich cells that constantly shed and renew. It protects the body from external harm.

The dermis underneath contains blood vessels, nerve endings, hair follicles, and sweat glands that support skin function. The hypodermis, also called subcutaneous tissue, is mostly fat and connective tissue. It cushions the body and helps regulate temperature by insulating internal organs.

Hair

Hair grows from follicles located in the dermis. It is made of keratin, the same protein in skin and nails. Hair helps protect the skin from UV radiation by shading it and trapping heat to insulate the body.

It also plays a sensory role, with nerve endings around follicles detecting slight movements or touch.

Nails

Nails are hard plates of keratin that cover the tips of fingers and toes. They protect these sensitive areas from injury and help with delicate tasks like picking things up or scratching.

Nails grow from a base called the matrix beneath the skin and continuously push older cells outward to form the visible nail.

Glands

The skin contains several important glands:

  • Eccrine sweat glands are found all over the body. They secrete mostly water and salt to help cool the body through evaporation.
  • Apocrine sweat glands are located mainly in the armpits and groin. They produce a thicker sweat that bacteria break down, causing body odor.
  • Sebaceous glands secrete oily sebum that lubricates and waterproofs the skin and hair, keeping them flexible and preventing dryness.
  • Ceruminous glands in the ear canal produce earwax, which traps dust and protects the ear.
  • Mammary glands, present in the chest area, produce milk in females for feeding babies.
